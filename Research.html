Research

Graduate

Under the mentorship of Dr. Susan E. Gresens and in collaboration with the Baltimore County Department of Environmental Protection and Sustainability (BC DEPS), my research seeks to understand how intense urban development in the past decades impacted Red Run watershed, a former trout watershed. My MS thesis consists of two chapters: (1) Red Run from 1987 to 2009; (2) Red Run after 2009.

First chapter: In the 1990s, there was an intense phase of urban development in the Baltimore-Washington area. Specifically, in Baltimore County, Owings Mills was among the two designated growth areas and Red Run watershed consisted of the majority of the development area. I collected land cover data from the USGS Land Change Monitoring, Assessment, and Projection and impervious surface area (ISA) data from the NLCD. I also used benthic macroinvertebrate and water chemistry data collected in 1987-1988 and 2009 by the BC DEPS. The objectives are to investigate (a) how land cover and water chemistry changed due to urban development, (b) how benthic macroinvertebrate composition responded, and (c) which stressor drove the change in benthic community.

Second chapter: After 2009, land cover was predicted to reach a stable state. The definition of the steady state of land cover was adopted from Bird et al. (2018), in which changes in different land cover categories are below 1% over a period of time. Despite urbanization slowing down, the question would be whether major ion concentrations and benthic communities would also become stable. Land cover data from the USGS Land Change Monitoring, Assessment, and Projection, ISA data from the NLCD, and benthic monitoring data from the BC DEPS were also used. In this chapter, I also incorporated benthic data from the Maryland Biological Stream Survey as part of the analyses. Further, I used long-term discharge data from USGS gauging station for Gwynns Fall at Villa Nova to relate to the short-term discharge data from another station in Red Run watershed and compare the hydrological pattern to other more forested watersheds nearby. The objectives are to investigate (a) how urban development continued to impact water chemistry, (b) how benthic macroinvertebrate compositions were affected, (c) whether metrics and IBI scores are effective in assessing biotic integrity, and (d) whether hydrology could be another factor impacting benthic communities.

Manuscripts from these chapters are in preparation!

Undergraduate

During my undergraduate career, under the supervisions of Mr. Jonathan Miller and Dr. Brian Helms, I have done a variety of projects on crayfish, mussels, and benthic macroinvertebrates. My past research greatly built up my research skills and sparked my interest in freshwater ecology.

The research that I was most proud of was our mussel study, in which we examined how long individuals of Fusconaia escambia, a federally listed species of mussel, survived under the drawdown conditions of the Gantt Reservoir. Our mussel study was also featured on Troy Today. Check out this link.

I am also currently working with the team to prepare a manuscript based on this research. Stay tuned!
